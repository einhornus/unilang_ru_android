This is useful to connect a <code>SelectionInList</code> with other <code>ValueModel</code>s; for example you can use the <code>SelectionInList</code>'s selection holder as bean channel for a <code>PresentationModel</code>. Since the <code>SelectionInList</code> is a <code>ValueModel</code>, it is often used as bean channel. See the Binding tutorial classes for examples on how to connect a <code>SelectionInList</code> with a <code>PresentationModel</code>.“</dd></dl></li></ul></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p><i>Beans</i> and <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">peas</i> are sometimes misidentified with one another; they are both legumes (belong to the family <i class="Latn mention" lang="mul">Fabaceae</i>) and seeds. <i>Bean</i> was later extended to other seeds belonging to the New World genus <i class="Latn mention" lang="mul">Phaseolus</i> (runner beans, lima beans etc.). Some other non-<i class="Latn mention" lang="mul">Fabaceae</i> plants (coffee, cocoa, vanilla, soy, ...) are also referred to as beans because of their resemblance to ordinary beans.
</p><p>Peas are a type of bean with smaller, round seeds in the pod, in contrast to oval or kidney-shaped seeds usually referred to as <i>beans</i>. Because both terms are applied to a wide range of different legumes the distinction is not always clear: <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">garbanzo bean</i> is a synonym of <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">chickpea</i>.
</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h5>
<ul><li>The past participle of <i>bear</i> is usually <i>borne</i>:
<ul><li><i>He could not have <b>borne</b> that load.</i></li>
<li><i>She had <b>borne</b> five children.</i></li>
<li><i>This is not to be <b>borne</b>!</i></li></ul></li>
<li>However, when <i>bear</i> means "to give birth to" (literally or figuratively), the passive past participle is <i>born</i>:
<ul><li><i>She <b>was born</b> on May 3.</i></li>
<li><i><b>Born</b> three years earlier, he was the eldest of his siblings.</i></li>
<li>"The idea to create [the Blue Ridge Parkway] <b>was born</b> in the travail of the Great Depression <span class="q-hellip-sp"> </span><span class="q-hellip-b">[</span>…<span class="q-hellip-b">]</span><span class="q-hellip-b"> </span>." (Tim Pegram, <i>The Blue Ridge Parkway by Foot: A Park Ranger's Memoir</i>, →ISBN, 2007, page 1)</li></ul></li>
<li>Both spellings have been used in the construction <i>born(e) into the world/family</i> and <i>born(e) to someone</i> (as a child). The <i>borne</i> spellings are more frequent in older and religious writings.
<ul><li><i>He was <b>born(e)</b> to Mr. Smith.</i></li>
<li><i>She was <b>born(e)</b> into the most powerful family in the city.</i></li>
<li>"[M]y father was <b>borne</b> to a Swedish mother and a Norwegian father, both devout Lutherans." (David Ross, <i>Good Morning Corfu: Living Abroad Against All Odds</i>, →ISBN, 2009)</li></ul></li>
<li>In some colloquial speech, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">beared</i> can be found for both the simple past and the past participle, although it is usually considered nonstandard and avoided in writing. Similarly, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">bore</i> may be extended to the past participle; the same provisos apply for this form.</li></ul>
