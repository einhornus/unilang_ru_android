<head> <meta charset='utf-8'> </head> <body><h1> chum (18453)</h1><br><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_1">Etymology 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p>1675–85; of uncertain origin, possibly from <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">cham</i>, shortening of <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">chambermate</i>, or from <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">comrade</i>. Less likely from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welsh_language" class="extiw" title="w:Welsh language">Welsh</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="cy">cymrawd</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">fellow</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>, compare <i class="Latn mention" lang="cy">brawd</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">brother</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>.
</p>
<hr><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">chum</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">chums</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>A friend; a pal.
<dl><dd><span class="nyms synonym"><span class="defdate">Synonyms:</span> <i>see</i> Thesaurus:<span class="Latn" lang="en">friend</span></span></dd>
<dd><div class="h-usage-example"><i class="Latn mention e-example" lang="en">I ran into an old <b>chum</b> from school the other day.</i></div></dd></dl></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">dated</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A roommate, especially in a college or university.
<ul><li><b>1856</b> in <i>The Knickerbocker: Or, New-York Monthly Magazine</i> <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://books.google.com/books?vid=LCCN01002996amp;id=N_dFtyzEqFsCamp;pg=PA161amp;lpg=PA161amp;dq=chumamp;as_brr=1">[1]</a>
<dl><dd>Field had a '<b>chum,'</b> or room-mate, whose visage was suggestive to the 'Sophs;' it invited experiment; it held out opportunity for their peculiar deviltry.</dd></dl></li></ul></li></ol>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p>This word has never been in common usage; Zamenhof advised against using 'ci' as early as the <i>Dua Libro de l' Lingvo Internacia</i>, published in 1888.  Some authors have used 'ci' to portray archaic language, for translations, and for stylistic effects.  This usage is criticized by other writers.
