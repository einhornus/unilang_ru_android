***tea.html
<head> <meta charset='utf-8'> </head> <body><h1> tea (1648)</h1><br><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_1">Etymology 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p>Circa 1650, from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_language" class="extiw" title="w:Dutch language">Dutch</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="nl">thee</i>, from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Min" class="extiw" title="w:Southern Min">Min Nan</a></span> <i class="Hani mention" lang="nan">茶</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span lang="nan-Latn" class="mention-tr tr Latn">tê</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span> (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amoy_dialect" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Amoy dialect">Amoy dialect</a>), from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chinese" class="extiw" title="w:Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a></span>, ultimately from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" class="extiw" title="w:Sino-Tibetan languages">Proto-Sino-Tibetan</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="sit-pro">*s-la</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">leaf, tea</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>.
</p><p>Introduced to English and other Western European languages by the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a>, who sourced their tea in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amoy" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Amoy">Amoy</a>; compare <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_language" class="extiw" title="w:Malay language">Malay</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="ms">teh</i> along the same trade route. Doublet of <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">chai</i> and <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">cha</i> (and, distantly, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">lahpet</i>), from same Proto-Sino-Tibetan root; see discussion of cognates.
</p>
<div class="NavFrame">
<div class="NavHead" style="text-align: left;">Cognates</div>
<div class="NavContent derivedterms" style="text-align: left; column-count: 2; -moz-column-count: 2; -ms-column-count: 2; -o-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2;">
<p>The word for “tea” in many languages is of Sinitic origin (due to China being the origin of the plant), and thus there are many cognates; see <a href="Translations">translations</a>. These are from one of two proximate sources, reflected in the phonological shape: forms with a stop (e.g. <span class="IPA">/t/</span>) are derived from Min Nan <i>tê</i>, while forms with a fricative (e.g. <span class="IPA">/tʃ/</span>) are derived from other Sinitic languages, like Mandarin <i>chá</i> or Cantonese <i>caa4</i> (all written as <span class="Hani" lang="zh">茶</span>). Different languages borrowed one or the other form (specific language and point in time varied), reflecting trade ties, generally Min Nan <i>tê</i> if by ocean trade from Fujian, Cantonese <i>caa4</i> if by ocean trade from Guangdong, or northern Chinese <i>chá</i> if by overland trade or by ocean trade from India.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-1"> | 1 | </a></sup>
</p><p>Thus Western and Northern European languages borrowed <i>tê</i> (with the exception of Portuguese, which uses <i class="Latn mention" lang="pt">chá</i>; despite being by ocean trade, their source was in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macao" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Macao">Macao</a>, not Amoy), while <i>chá</i> borrowings are used over a very large geographical area of Eurasia and Africa: Southern and Eastern Europe, and on through Turkish, Arabic, North and East Africa, Persian, Central Asian, and Indic languages. In Europe the <i>tê</i>/<i>chá</i> line is Italian/Slovene, Hungarian/Romanian, German/Czech, Polish/Ukrainian, Baltics/Russian, Finnish/Karelian, Northern Sami/Inari Sami. <i>tê</i> was also borrowed in European trade stops in Southern India and coastal Africa, though <i>chá</i> borrowings are otherwise more prevalent in these regions, via Arabic or Indic, due to earlier trade. The situation in Southeast Asia is complex due to multiple influences, and some languages borrowed both forms, such as Malay <i class="Latn mention" lang="ms">teh</i> and <i class="Latn mention" lang="ms">ca</i>.
</p>

<hr><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_2">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">tea</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">teas</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>A moment, a historical unit of time from China, about the amount of time needed to quickly drink a traditional cup of tea. It is now found in Chinese-language historical fiction.</li></ol>
