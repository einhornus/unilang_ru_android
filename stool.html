stool (8890)


Etymology 1

From Middle English stool, stole, stol, from Old English stōl (“chair, seat, throne”), from Proto-Germanic *stōlaz (“chair”) (compare West Frisian stoel, Dutch stoel, German Stuhl, Swedish/Norwegian/Danish stol, Finnish tuoli, Estonian tool), from Proto-Indo-European *stoh₂los (compare Lithuanian stálas, Russian стол (stol, “table”), Russian стул (stul, “chair”), Serbo-Croatian stol (“table”), Slovene stol (“chair”), Albanian kështallë (“crutch”), Ancient Greek στήλη (stḗlē, “block of stone used as a prop or buttress to a wall”)), from *steh₂- (“to stand”). More at stand.

The medical use derives from sense 4 (seat used for defecation).

Noun

stool (countable and uncountable, plural stools)

  1. A seat for one person without a back or armrest.
    1. A footstool.
    2. (now chiefly dialectal, Scotland, literally and figurative) A throne.
