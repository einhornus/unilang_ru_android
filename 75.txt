</p><p>The traditional etymology as a derivation from the Proto-Indo-European verb for 'to dig' has been doubted, arguing that there are (allegedly) few, if any, cultures known to dig out beds, rather than to build "pads". However, what the Germanic word originally referred to is not known with precision, and it notably has the additional meaning "flower-bed, plot", which is preserved in English and several other modern Germanic languages, but present in older stages as well. Additionally, the term may have originally been used in the sense of a "burial plot" for laying those who were asleep in death, and from there extended also to symbolise a place where one slept in general (In Modern German, two separate words exist, <i>Bett</i> being the normal term, the rare variant <i class="Latn mention" lang="de">Beet</i> having been adopted for “flower-bed”). Perhaps the word originally referred to dug sleeping-places of animals, compare (with the inverse semantic development) <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">lair</i> from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English" class="extiw" title="w:Old English">Old English</a></span> <i class="Latinx mention" lang="ang">leġer</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">couch, bed</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p>Sense 1. To prepare a bed is usually <i>to "make" the bed</i>, or <span class="ib-brac qualifier-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content qualifier-content">US<span class="ib-comma qualifier-comma">,</span> Southern</span><span class="ib-brac qualifier-brac">)</span> to "spread" the bed, the verb spread probably having been developed from bedspread.
Like many nouns denoting places where people spend time, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">bed</i> requires no article after certain prepositions: hence <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">in bed</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">lying in a bed</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">go to bed</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">get into a bed</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>, and so on. The forms <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">in a bed</i>, etc. do exist, but tend to imply mere presence in the bed, without it being for the purpose of sleep.
