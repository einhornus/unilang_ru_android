<head> <meta charset='utf-8'> </head> <body><h1> lai (15205)</h1><br><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_7">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">lai</i> is derived from the imperative form of the verb <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">laist</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">to allow, to permit</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span> (quod vide): <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">laid</i> → <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">lai</i>, an evolution reminiscent of <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Russian language">Russian</a></span> <i class="Cyrl mention" lang="ru"><a href = vnd.unilang:пускать.html>пуска́й</a></i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span lang="ru-Latn" class="mention-tr tr Latn">puskaj</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>, <i class="Cyrl mention" lang="ru"><a href = vnd.unilang:пусть.html>пусть</a></i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span lang="ru-Latn" class="mention-tr tr Latn">pustʹ</span>, <span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">let, so be</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span> from <i class="Cyrl mention" lang="ru"><a href = vnd.unilang:пускать.html>пуска́ть</a></i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span lang="ru-Latn" class="mention-tr tr Latn">puskatʹ</span>, <span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">to allow, to permit</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>. The form <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">laid</i> is indeed attested as a conjunction in the earliest sources. This view, however, has been recently criticized on the basis that <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Latvian language">Latvian</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">lai</i>, <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuanian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="lt">laĩ</i> are clearly related to <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Prussian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Old Prussian language">Old Prussian</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="prg">-lai</i>, which is added to (usually infinitive) verbs to indicate volitive or conditional mood. This suggests a <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Balto-Slavic_language" class="extiw" title="w:Proto-Balto-Slavic language">Proto-Baltic</a></span> form <i class="Latinx mention" lang="ine-bsl-pro">*lai</i>, probably related to the final <i>-le</i> of Latvian reinforcing particles <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">jele</i> ~ <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">jel</i>, <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">nule</i>, and (dialectal) <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">nele</i>, and to <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Prussian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Old Prussian language">Old Prussian</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="prg">-le</i>, apparently a variant of <i class="Latn mention" lang="prg">-lai</i>. This <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">*le</i> would then have the same origin as <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Slavic" class="extiw" title="w:Proto-Slavic">Proto-Slavic</a></span> <i class="Latinx mention" lang="sla-pro">*li</i> (compare <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_language" class="extiw" title="w:Polish language">Polish</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="pl">li</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">only</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span> <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language" class="extiw" title="w:Russian language">Russian</a></span> <i class="Cyrl mention" lang="ru"><a href = vnd.unilang:ли.html>ли</a></i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span lang="ru-Latn" class="mention-tr tr Latn">li</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>), with cognates in other languages (Albanian, Tocharian) from a basic <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" class="extiw" title="w:Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a></span> <i class="Latinx mention" lang="ine-pro">*l-</i>. After this criticism, the relationship between <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">lai</i> and <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">laist</i> has become unclear. Maybe <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" class="extiw" title="w:Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a></span> <i class="Latinx mention" lang="ine-pro">*l-</i> was an old verb, or maybe <i class="Latn mention" lang="lv">laist</i> was derived from an older particle.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-1"> | 1 | </a></sup>
</p>
