<p>Partly from post-classical <span class="etylcleanup"><span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin" class="extiw" title="w:Latin">Latin</a></span></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="la">terminator</i> (5th century), from <span class="etylcleanup"><span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin" class="extiw" title="w:Latin">Latin</a></span></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="la">terminō</i>; partly from <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">terminate</i> + |  <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">-or</i>.

<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">terminator</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">terminators</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>Someone who terminates or ends something, especially (in later use) an assassin or exterminator. <span class="defdate">[from 17th c.]</span></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">astronomy</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> The line between the day side and the night side of a moon, planet or other celestial body. <span class="defdate">[from 17th c.]</span>
<ul><li><b>2015</b>, David Wootton, <i>The Invention of Science</i>, Penguin 2016, p. 218:
<dl><dd>Harriot, looking at the moon, saw the irregular <b>terminator</b>, the highlights and shadows, the mountain ranges and valleys that Galileo had described – and he also convinced himself that he saw Galileo's imaginary crater.</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">biochemistry</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A DNA sequence which causes RNA transcription to cease and an MRNA transcript to break off. <span class="defdate">[from 20th c.]</span></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">electronics</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> An electrical device that absorbs reflection at the end of a transmission line.</li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">science fiction</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> An intelligent android created to destroy humans (after the 1984 film <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Terminator" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:The Terminator">The Terminator</a></i>).</li></ol>

<head> <meta charset='utf-8'> </head> <body><h1> tesla (13170)</h1><br><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p>Named after <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" class="extiw" title="w:Nikola Tesla">Nikola Tesla</a>; where Nikola means Nicholas, and Tesla is a Serbian surname. In Serbian Cyrillic: Никола Тесла
</p>
<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">tesla</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">teslas</b> <i>or</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en"><strong class="selflink">tesla</strong></b>) <span class="ib-brac qualifier-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content qualifier-content">see usage notes</span><span class="ib-brac qualifier-brac">)</span>
</p>
<ol><li>In the International System of Units, the derived unit of magnetic flux density or magnetic inductivity. Symbol: T
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>2015</b> August 18,  John Timmer,  “Small, modular nuke plant proposed—this time for fusion”, in  <cite>Ars Technica</cite> | <sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2015/08/small-modular-nuke-plant-proposed-this-time-for-fusion/">[1]</a></sup>:</span><dl><dd></dd></dl></div>
<dl><dd>In a draft paper, REBCO wiring has been reported to produce magnetic fields over 35 <b>Tesla</b>; the ARC design only needs 20T fields.</dd></dl></li></ul></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<ul><li>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.bipm.org/utils/common/pdf/si-brochure/SI-Brochure-9.pdf">SI brochure</a> (Sec 5.3) notes that <i>[u]nit names are normally printed in upright type and they are treated like ordinary nouns.</i> This includes the normal rules of plural formation.</li>
<li>The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://physics.nist.gov/cuu/pdf/sp811.pdf">NIST Guide for the Use of SI</a> (Sec. 9.2) states: <i>Plural  unit  names  are  used  when  they  are  required  by  the  rules  of  English  grammar.  They  are  normally  formed  regularly,  for  example,  “henries”  is  the  plural  of  henry.  According  to  <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1160090/metrics">Ref. [6</a>],  the  following  plurals  are  irregular:  Singular —lux,  hertz,  siemens;  Plural —lux,  hertz,  siemens.  (See  also  Sec. 9.7.)</i> There is no special exception for <i>tesla.</i></li>
<li>When using the unit name <i>tesla</i> as an adjective, it is normally not pluralized.  Compare the following:</li></ul>
<ol><li>The nine-<i>volt</i> battery provides a potential difference of nine <i>volts</i>.</li>
<li>The two-<b>tesla</b> magnet produces a flux density of two <b>teslas</b>.</li></ol>
<ul><li>Despite this distinction, the nonstandard plural forms <i>Tesla</i> or <i>tesla</i> are frequently found in scientific and lay literature. (See above quotation.)</li></ul>
