<p>From <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_English" class="extiw" title="w:Middle English">Middle English</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="enm">revolucion</i>, borrowed from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_French" class="extiw" title="w:Old French">Old French</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="fro">revolucion</i>, from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Latin" class="extiw" title="w:Late Latin">Late Latin</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="la">revolūtiōnem</i>, accusative singular of <i class="Latn mention" lang="la">revolūtiō</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">the act of revolving; revolution</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>, from <span class="etyl"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin" class="extiw" title="w:Latin">Latin</a></span> <i class="Latn mention" lang="la">revolvō</i> <span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">(</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">“</span><span class="mention-gloss">roll back, revolve</span><span class="mention-gloss-double-quote">”</span><span class="mention-gloss-paren annotation-paren">)</span>.
</p>
<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">revolution</strong> (<i>countable and uncountable</i>, <i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">revolutions</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>A political upheaval in a government or nation state characterized by great change.</li>
<li>The removal and replacement of a government, especially by sudden violent action.
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1837</b>,  <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Sand" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:George Sand">George Sand</a>, Stanley Young, transl., <cite><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauprat" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Mauprat">Mauprat</a></cite> | <sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://books.google.co.nz/books?id=NTTeAwAAQBAJ">[1]</a></sup>, Cassandra Editions, published 1977, <small>→ISBN</small>, page 237:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">For a long time the dormouse and polecat had seemed to him overfeeble enemies for his restless valour, even as the granary floor seemed to afford too narrow a field. Every day he read the papers of the previous day in the servants' hall of the houses he visited, and it appeared to him that this war in America, which was hailed as the awakening of the spirit of liberty and justice in the New World, ought to produce a <b>revolution</b> in France.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
<li>Rotation: the turning of an object around an axis.
<ul><li><b>1912</b>, P. M. Heldt, <i>The Gasoline Automobile: Its Design and Construction, Volume II: Transmission, Running Gear and Control</i>, The Horseless Age Co. (1913), <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=gqo7AAAAMAAJamp;pg=PA147amp;dq=revolution">page 147</a>:
<dl><dd>The ratio between the speeds of <b>revolution</b> of wheel and disc is substantially equal to the reciprocal of the ratio between the diameter of the wheel and the diameter of the mean contact circle on the disc.</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li>A rotation: one complete turn of an object during rotation.
<ul><li><b>1864</b>, D. M. Warren, <i>The Common-School Geography</i>, Revised Edition, H. Cowperthwait amp; Co., <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=meI-AAAAYAAJamp;pg=PA6amp;dq=revolution">page 6</a>:
<dl><dd>The Earth has two motions: a daily <b>revolution</b> (or turning around) upon its <i>axis</i>, and a yearly course around the sun.</dd></dl></li>
<li><b>1878</b>, George Fleming, <i>A Text-Book of Veterinary Obstetrics</i>, Baillière, Tindall, amp; Cox, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=MUQDAAAAQAAJamp;pg=PA123amp;dq=revolution">page 123</a>:
<dl><dd>Numerous cases are recorded which incontestibly prove that during pregnancy, the uterus perform  a half or even a complete <b>revolution</b>, on itself, producing torsion of the cervix <span class="q-hellip-sp"> </span><span class="q-hellip-b">[</span>…<span class="q-hellip-b">]</span><span class="q-hellip-b"> </span></dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li>In the case of celestial bodies - the traversal of one body through an orbit around another body.</li>
<li>A sudden, vast change in a situation, a discipline, or the way of thinking and behaving.</li>
<li>A round of periodic changes, such as between the seasons of the year.</li>
<li>Consideration of an idea; the act of revolving something in the mind.</li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<ul><li>Astronomers today do not use <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">revolution</i> to refer to the turning of an object about an axis: they use <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">rotation</i> for that, and <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">revolution</i> only for the traversal of a body through an orbit (which also happens around some axis). (This may be somewhat customary, however, strictly speaking, using either word for either process would not be incorrect.)</li></ul>
