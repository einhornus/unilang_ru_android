<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">apple</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">apples</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>A common, round fruit produced by the tree <i>Malus domestica</i>, cultivated in temperate climates. <span class="defdate">[from 9th c.]</span>
<ul><li><b>c. 1378</b>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Langland" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:William Langland">William Langland</a>, <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piers_Plowman" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Piers Plowman">Piers Plowman</a></i>:
<dl><dd>I prayed pieres to pulle adown an <b>apple</b>.</dd></dl></li>
<li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1815</b>,  <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Austen" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>,  <cite>Emma</cite>:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Not that I had any doubt before – I have so often heard Mr. Woodhouse recommend a baked <b>apple</b>.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li>
<li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>2013</b> October 28,  John Vallins,  “Apples of concord”, in  <cite>The Guardian</cite> | <sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2013/oct/28/somerset-apples-of-concord">[1]</a></sup>:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Close by and under cover, I watched the juicing process. <b>Apples</b> were washed, then tipped, stalks and all, into the crusher and reduced to pulp.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
<li>Any of various tree-borne fruits or vegetables especially considered as resembling an apple; also (with qualifying words) used to form the names of other specific fruits such as <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">custard apple</i>, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">rose apple</i>, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">thorn apple</i> etc. <span class="defdate">[from 9th c.]</span>
<ul><li><b>1658</b>, trans. Giambattista della Porta, <i>Natural Magick</i>, I.16:
<dl><dd>In Persia there grows a deadly tree, whose <b>Apples</b> are Poison, and present death.</dd></dl></li>
<li><b>1784</b>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cook" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:James Cook">James Cook</a>, <i>A Voyage to the Pacific Ocean</i>, II:
<dl><dd>Otaheite […] is remarkable for producing great quantities of that delicious fruit we called <b>apples</b>, which are found in none of the others, except Eimeo.</dd></dl></li>
<li><b>1825</b>, Theodric Romeyn Beck, <i>Elements of Medical Jurisprudence</i>, 2nd edition, page 565:
<dl><dd><i>Hippomane mancinella.</i> (Manchineel-tree.) Dr. Peysonnel relates that a soldier, who was a slave with the Turks, eat some of the <b>apples</b> of this tree, and was soon seized with a swelling and pain of the abdomen.</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li>The fruit of the Tree of Knowledge, eaten by Adam and Eve according to Post-Biblical Christian tradition; the forbidden fruit. <span class="defdate">[from 11th c.]</span>
<ul><li><b>1667</b>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Milton" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:John Milton">John Milton</a>, <i>Paradise Lost</i>, Book X:
<dl><dd>Him by fraud I have seduced / From his Creator; and, the more to encrease / Your wonder, with an <b>apple</b> […].</dd></dl></li>
<li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1985</b>,  <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Reckord" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Barry Reckord">Barry Reckord</a>,  <cite>The White Witch</cite>:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Woman ate the <b>apple</b>, and discovered sex, and lost all shame, and lift up her fig—leaf, and she must suffer the pains of hell. Monthly.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
<li>A tree of the genus <i>Malus</i>, especially one cultivated for its edible fruit; the apple tree. <span class="defdate">[from 15th c.]</span>
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1913</b>,  John Weathers,  <cite>Commercial Gardening</cite>, page 38:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">If the grafted portion of an <b>Apple</b> or other tree were examined after one hundred years, the old cut surfaces would still be present, for mature or ripened wood, being dead, never unites.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li>
<li><b>2000</b> PA Thomas, <i>Trees: Their Natural History</i>, page 227:
<dl><dd>This allows a weak plant to benefit from the strong roots of another, or a vigorous tree (such as an <b>apple</b>) to be kept small by growing on 'dwarfing rootstock'.</dd></dl></li>
<li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>2009</b>,  Sid Gardner,  <cite>The Faults of the Owens Valley</cite>, <small>→ISBN</small>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=gcbQzBOnc0YCamp;pg=PA34amp;dq=%22watered+the+apples%22">page 34</a>:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Used to be apple orchards, used to be the river and irrigation ditches that watered the <b>apples</b>, used to be mining towns.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li>

<li>The wood of the apple tree. <span class="defdate">[from 19th c.]</span></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">in the plural<span class="ib-comma">,</span> Cockney rhyming slang</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> Short for <b>apples and pears</b>, slang for stairs. <span class="defdate">[from 20th c.]</span></li>
