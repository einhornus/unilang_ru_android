<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">malt</strong> (<i>countable and uncountable</i>, <i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">malts</b>)
</p>
<ol><li><a href="Verb">Malted</a> grain <span class="gloss-brac">(</span><span class="gloss-content">sprouted grain</span><span class="gloss-brac">)</span> (usually barley), used in brewing and otherwise.</li>
<li>Malt liquor, especially malt whisky.
<ul><li><b>1896</b>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._E._Housman" class="extiw" title="w:A. E. Housman">A. E. Housman</a>, <i>A Shropshire Lad</i>, LXII:
<dl><dd>Oh many a peer of England brews</dd>
<dd>Livelier liquor than the Muse,</dd>
<dd>And <b>malt</b> does more than Milton can</dd>
<dd>To justify God's ways to man.</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States" class="extiw" title="w:United States">US</a><span class="ib-comma">,</span> informal</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A milkshake with malted milk powder added for flavor.
<dl><dd><span class="nyms synonym"><span class="defdate">Synonym:</span> <span class="Latn" lang="en">malted</span></span></dd></dl></li>
<li>Maltose-rich sugar derived from malted grain.</li></ol>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h5>
<ul><li>The use of “man” (compare Old English: <span class="Latinx" lang="ang">mann</span>, <span class="Latinx" lang="ang">wer</span>, <span class="Latinx" lang="ang">wīf</span>) to mean both “human (of any gender)” and “adult male”, which developed after Old English’s distinct term for the latter (<i class="Latinx mention" lang="ang">wer</i>) fell out of use, has been criticized since at least the second half of the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxforddict_1-0" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-Oxforddict-1"> | 1 | </a></sup> Critics claim that the use of “man”, both alone and in compounds, to denote a human or any gender “is now often regarded as sexist or at best old-fashioned”,<sup id="cite_ref-Oxforddict_1-1" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-Oxforddict-1"> | 1 | </a></sup> “flatly discriminatory in that it slights or ignores the membership of women in the human race”.<sup id="cite_ref-Dict_2-0" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-Dict-2"> | 2 | </a></sup> The <i>American Heritage Dictionary</i> wrote that in 2004 75-79% of their usage panel still accepted sentences with generic <i>man</i>, and 86-87% accepted sentences with <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">man-made</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-3"> | 3 | </a></sup> Some style guides recommend against generic “man”,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-4"> | 4 | </a></sup> and “although some editors and writers reject or disregard [...] objections to man as a generic, many now choose instead to use” <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">human</i>, <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">human being</i> or <i class="Latn mention" lang="en">person</i> instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Dict_2-1" class="reference"><a href="cite_note-Dict-2"> | 2 | </a></sup>
<ul><li>This generic usage is still well-preserved in certain dialects, pidgins, and creoles of English, as well as fixed expressions and certain religious documents and declarations such as the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicene_Creed" class="extiw" title="w:Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a> (e.g. "...for us <b>men</b> and our salvation..."). Consideration of this has often led to accusations of the critics of the generic <i>man</i> as enforcing linguistic prescriptivism.</li></ul></li>
<li>See also <b><i class="Latn mention" lang="en">the man</i></b></li></ul>
