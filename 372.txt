<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg/220px-Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="115" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg/330px-Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg/440px-Canada_goose_flight_cropped_and_NR.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1440" data-file-height="750" />  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"></div>a flying goose</div></div></div>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">goose</strong> (<i>countable and uncountable</i>, <i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">geese</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>Any of various grazing waterfowl of the family Anatidae, which have feathers and webbed feet and are capable of flying, swimming, and walking on land, and which are bigger than ducks.
<dl><dd><i>There is a flock of <b>geese</b> on the pond.</i></dd></dl></li>
<li>The flesh of the goose used as food.
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1843</b>,  Charles Dickens,  “<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/A_Christmas_Carol_(Dickens)/Stave_3" class="extiw" title="s:A Christmas Carol (Dickens)/Stave 3">Stave 3: The Second of the Three Spirits</a>”, in  <cite>A Christmas Carol</cite>:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Mrs. Cratchit made the gravy (ready beforehand in a little saucepan) hissing hot; Master Peter mashed the potatoes with incredible vigour; Miss Belinda sweetened up the apple-sauce; Martha dusted the hot plates; Bob took Tiny Tim beside him in a tiny corner at the table; the two young Cratchits set chairs for everybody, not forgetting themselves, and mounting guard upon their posts, crammed spoons into their mouths, lest they should shriek for <b>goose</b> before their turn came to be helped.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">slang</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A silly person.
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1906</b>,  Langdon Mitchell,  “The New York Idea”, in John Gassner, editor, <cite>Best Plays of the Early American Theatre, 1787-1911</cite> | <sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=xVSJZKaX3ScC">[1]</a></sup>, published 2000, <small>→ISBN</small>, page 430:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">I'm sorry for you, but you're such a <b>goose</b>.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">archaic</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A tailor's iron, heated in live coals or embers, used to press fabrics.
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><i><abbr title="circa">c.</abbr></i> <b>1606</b>,  <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shakespeare" class="extiw" title="w:William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>,  “<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macbeth" class="extiw" title="w:Macbeth">The Tragedie of Macbeth</a>”, in  <cite>Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, amp; Tragedies: Published According to the True Originall Copies</cite> (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Folio" class="extiw" title="w:First Folio">First Folio</a>), London |  Printed by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Jaggard" class="extiw" title="w:William Jaggard">Isaac Iaggard</a>, and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Blount" class="extiw" title="w:Edward Blount">Ed[ward] Blount</a>, published 1623, <small><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OCLC" class="extiw" title="w:OCLC">OCLC</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://worldcat.org/oclc/606515358">606515358</a></small>, [Act II, scene iii]:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Come in, tailor. <li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa" class="extiw" title="w:South Africa">South Africa</a><span class="ib-comma">,</span> slang<span class="ib-comma">,</span> dated</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> A young woman or girlfriend.</li>
<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">uncountable<span class="ib-comma">,</span> historical</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> An old English board game in which players moved counters along a board, earning a double move when they reached the picture of a goose.</li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<ul><li>A male goose is called a gander. A young goose is a gosling.</li>
<li>A group of geese can be called a gaggle when they are on the ground or in the water, and a skein or a wedge when they are in flight.</li></ul>

<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">goose</strong> (<i>third-person singular simple present</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en 3 | s | pres-form-of" lang="en">gooses</b>, <i>present participle</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en pres | ptcp-form-of" lang="en">goosing</b>, <i>simple past and past participle</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en past | and | past | ptcp-form-of" lang="en">goosed</b>)
