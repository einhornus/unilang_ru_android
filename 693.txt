<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">protocol</strong> (<i>countable and uncountable</i>, <i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">protocols</b>)
</p>
<ol><li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">now  chiefly  historical</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> The minutes, or official record, of a negotiation or transaction; especially a document drawn up officially which forms the legal basis for subsequent agreements based on it. <span class="defdate">[from 15th c.]</span>

<li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content">international law<span class="ib-comma">,</span> now  rare</span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> An official record of a diplomatic meeting or negotiation; later specifically, a draft document setting out agreements to be signed into force by a subsequent formal treaty. <span class="defdate">[from 17th c.]</span>
<ul><li><b>1970</b>, Matthew Smith Anderson, <i>The Great Powers and the Near East, 1774-1923</i>, p. 32:

<ul><li><b>1991</b>, Leila Avrin, <i>Scribes, Script, and Books</i>, p. 146:
<dl><dd>They marked the beginning of each scroll with their <i><b>protocol</b></i>, a practice that continued in the papyrus trade in the Byzantine Empire [...] into the Islamic period, when there were bilingual <b>protocols</b> in Greek and Arabic.</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li>The official formulas which appeared at the beginning or end of certain official documents such as charters, papal bulls etc. <span class="defdate">[from 19th c.]</span>

<li>The official rules and guidelines for heads of state and other dignitaries, governing accepted behaviour in relations with other diplomatic representatives or over affairs of state. <span class="defdate">[from 19th c.]</span>
