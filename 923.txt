</p>
<hr><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun">Noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h3>
<p><strong class="Latn headword" lang="en">weekend</strong> (<i>plural</i> <b class="Latn form-of lang-en p-form-of" lang="en">weekends</b>)
</p>
<ol><li>The break in the working week, usually two days including the traditional holy or sabbath day. Thus in western countries, Saturday and Sunday.
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1903</b>,  Francis Markham |  Sir Clements Robert Markham,  <cite>Recollections of a town boy at Westminster, 1849-1855</cite>, page 34:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">... often took a few boys down there for what we North Country folk call the <b>weekend</b> — Saturday and Sunday; it was also used as a sanatorium if required.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_notes">Usage notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"></span></h4>
<p>Historically in North America and parts of Europe, people would often work on Saturday as well, or at least until noon on Saturday. Thus the “weekend” might begin at noon or later on Saturday in older texts.
</p><p>To describe the soonest upcoming weekend:
</p>
<ul><li><span class="ib-brac">(</span><span class="ib-content"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain" class="extiw" title="w:Great Britain">Britain</a><span class="ib-comma">,</span> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand" class="extiw" title="w:New Zealand">New Zealand</a></span><span class="ib-brac">)</span> “at the weekend”, “on the weekend”, “this weekend”, “for the weekend”
<ul><li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>1886</b>,  New Zealand Parliament,  “Parliamentary debates”, in <span class="maintenance-line" style="color: 777777;">(Please provide the book title or journal name)</span>, volume 324, page 2371:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Let them work at their ordinary jobs during the week, and then take them out of circulation <b>at the weekend</b>, which is usually the time when the trouble is ...</span></div></dd></dl></div></li>
<li><div class="citation-whole"><span class="cited-source"><b>2009</b>,  Great Britain House of Commons: Business and Enterprise Committee,  “Pre-appointment Hearing with the Chairman-elect of Ofcom, Dr. Colette Bowe”, in <span class="maintenance-line" style="color: 777777;">(Please provide the book title or journal name)</span>, page 16:</span><dl><dd><div class="h-quotation"><span class="Latn e-quotation" lang="en">Whether it is on the BBC, ITV or commercial radio does not really matter. ...can give you a radio example of two things I was listening to <b>at the weekend</b>.</span></div></dd></dl></div></li></ul></li>
